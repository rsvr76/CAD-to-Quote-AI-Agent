# Step 6 - Confirming the Material Weight (Grandma Version)

---

## What are we actually doing here?

In Step 5, the system estimated how much raw material the part needs. Now it shows that estimate to the engineer and asks: "Does this look right?"

If the engineer already corrected the number in Step 5, this step is just a final double-check.

It's like a waiter repeating your order back to you before sending it to the kitchen.

---

## Why ask the engineer to confirm?

Because the system's estimate is based on geometry with margins. In reality:

- The factory might have standard-sized bars or plates that don't exactly match
- They might use leftover material from a previous job
- The supplier might only sell certain sizes

The engineer knows the real stock better than any formula.

---

## What happens?

```
System: Estimated stock
        Shape: rectangular block (turning OFF)
        Dimensions: 130 × 50 × 40 mm
        Weight: 1.6 kg
        Does this match your stock? [Accept / Override]

Option A: Engineer clicks Accept -> 1.6 kg is used
Option B: Engineer types 1.8 kg -> 1.8 kg is used instead

If the engineer overrides the estimate, we tag it as an **Engineer override**.
```

If the engineer changes the weight a lot compared to the estimate, the system shows a warning like:

"This is very different from the estimate — please double-check the stock size and units."

---

## What does this step produce?

A final confirmed stock weight (in kg) that goes directly into the material cost formula:

Material cost = weight x price per kg x volume discount

This is the last piece of human input before the system calculates the full quote.

Important architecture note (judge-friendly): the final stock weight is a fixed input to the cost calculation — it is **not** something the ML model predicts.
