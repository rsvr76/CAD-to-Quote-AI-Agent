# Step 5 - Figuring Out the Raw Material Needed (Grandma Version)

---

## What are we actually doing here?

Before you can machine a part, you need a chunk of raw material to start with. This step figures out how big that chunk needs to be and how much it weighs.

Think of it like sculpture: to carve a statue, you need a block of marble that's bigger than the final statue. We're figuring out how big that block needs to be.

---

## How does it figure out the block size?

It depends on whether the part needs turning (lathe work) or not:

- **If turning is needed** (long, thin part): Start with a round bar (like a metal rod)
- **If no turning** (normal shape): Start with a rectangular block (like a metal brick)

The system takes the part's dimensions and adds a small margin (extra material for clamping and safety).

If turning is needed, we assume the starting material is a **round bar** even if some milling is also needed later (for example, a turned shaft with a milled flat).

---

## How does it calculate weight?

Weight = Volume x Density

Different materials have very different densities:
- **Aluminium** is light (like a soda can)
- **Steel** is medium (like a car body)
- **Titanium** is surprisingly light for its strength
- **Brass** is heavy (like a door handle)

---

## Example

```
Part bounding box: 120mm x 40mm x 30mm
Shape: Not elongated, so rectangular block
Block size: 130mm x 50mm x 40mm (with margins)
Material: Steel
Weight: about 2 kg
```

---

## Why does this matter?

Because material cost = weight x price per kilogram. Heavier parts or expensive materials (like Titanium at ₹3200/kg) cost much more in raw material.

The system can also show a simple “waste” estimate for transparency:

- Part volume vs stock volume
- Approx waste % (how much material will be cut away)

We don’t model scrap resale/reuse value in the hackathon version.

---

## What does this step produce?

An estimated stock weight in kilograms.

The dashboard also lets the engineer **change (override)** this number right away if they know the real stock will be different (for example, standard bar sizes or leftover material). The system then uses the **final, engineer-confirmed** weight for material cost.

If the engineer overrides it, we tag it as an **Engineer override**.

One realism note: this estimate uses the part’s bounding box, which can be slightly inflated if the CAD model is rotated. That’s another reason the engineer override exists.
