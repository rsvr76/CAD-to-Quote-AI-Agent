# Step 3 - Asking the Engineer (Grandma Version)

---

## What are we actually doing here?

The system had a conversation with the engineer to collect information it couldn't figure out on its own from the 3D file. Think of it like a doctor asking you questions after looking at your X-ray - the X-ray shows the bones, but the doctor still needs to ask "where does it hurt?"

---

## What does the system ask?

Mostly confirmations + a few inputs:

1. **Holes:** "I detected 6 holes — is that correct?"
2. **Pockets:** "I detected 2 pockets — is that correct?"
3. **Depth:** "Max hole depth looks like ~25mm — confirm"
4. **What material?** - Steel, Aluminium, Titanium, Brass, or Plastic
5. **How many do you need?** - Ordering 1 vs 100 changes the setup cost dramatically
6. **How precise does it need to be?** - Standard, Fine, or Ultra-Fine tolerance

---

## Why can't the system figure this out automatically?

Even with STEP (which is much richer than STL), real engineering intent can be ambiguous. A round cylinder might be a hole, a boss, or a cosmetic cut. The engineer confirms the truth.

The engineer is the expert on their own design. Their input always overrides any computer guess.

If the engineer changes what the system guessed, we tag it as an **Engineer override**.

The system can also show a simple confidence hint like “holes detected: 6 (confidence: medium)” so the engineer knows what to double-check.

One small clarity note: “max_depth_mm” means the **deepest hole depth** (the deepest drilling), because that affects drilling time.

And we don’t accept nonsense inputs (like -3 holes or 0 quantity). The system validates values before continuing.

---

## Example conversation

```
System: I detected 6 holes from the STEP model. Is this correct?
Engineer: No, it has 6 holes.
```

The system accepts 6, not 4.

---

## What does this step produce?

A confirmed set of manufacturing details that, combined with the automatic measurements from Step 2, gives us everything we need to estimate the cost.
