# Step 2 - Measuring the Shape (Grandma Version)

---

## What are we actually doing here?

Now that we have a clean STEP CAD model, the system automatically measures it — like taking a ruler to a physical object, but digitally.

We do this because the AI can’t work directly with a 3D shape — it needs a compact set of numbers that correlate with machining effort.

---

## What does it measure?

| Measurement | In plain words |
|---|---|
| Volume | How much space the part takes up inside (like how much water it would displace) |
| Surface area | How much of the outside surface exists (like how much paint you'd need to cover it) |
| Bounding box | The smallest box that could fit around the part - its length, width, and height |
| Aspect ratio | Is the part long and thin (like a pen) or short and fat (like a dice)? |
| Surface-to-volume ratio | Is the part thin-walled (like a can) or a solid block (like a brick)? |
| Slenderness | How stick-like is the part relative to its overall size? |
| Face count | Roughly how many surfaces the CAD model has (complexity clue) |
| Edge count | Roughly how many edges the CAD model has (complexity clue) |

---

## Why do we need these measurements?

Each measurement tells us something about how hard (and expensive) the part will be to manufacture:

- **Large surface area** = more material to cut away = more machining time
- **Long and thin (high aspect ratio)** = probably needs a lathe (turning machine)
- **Thin-walled** = more delicate to machine, harder to hold

---

## What CAN'T the system measure from the file?

With STEP, the computer can often recognize features better than STL. But we still treat these as “confirm with the engineer” items:


- **Hole count** (some shapes look like holes but aren’t)
- **Pocket count** (some concave areas are cosmetic)
- **Max depth** (important for drilling time)

So in Step 3, we show what we detected and ask the engineer to confirm or correct it.

One more realism note: STEP files can be authored in different units (mm, cm, meters, inches). For hackathon scope we assume mm (common default), and we normalize units when possible.

---

## What does this step produce?

A table of measurements that describes the part's shape, shown to the engineer on screen so they can verify the numbers look correct.
