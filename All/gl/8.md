# Step 8 - The AI Makes Its Prediction (Grandma Version)

---

## What are we actually doing here?

We hand the list of 17 numbers from Step 7 to the AI model (Random Forest) and ask: "How many minutes will it take to machine this part?"

One important detail: the list must be in the **same fixed order** every time (the same “slot” must always mean the same feature). That’s how we avoid silent ML mistakes.

It's like showing a photo of a meal to an experienced chef and asking "how long would this take to cook?" The chef looks at the ingredients, complexity, and cooking methods, and gives you a time estimate.

---

## What does the AI predict?

**Machining time in minutes** - NOT the cost in rupees.

This is an important design choice. The AI only predicts how long the machines need to run. We convert time to money separately using a simple formula:

```
Cost = time x (selected machine rate per hour)
```

---

## Why predict time instead of cost?

Because machine rates can change (new equipment, inflation, different factories) without needing to retrain the AI. The AI learns the physics (how long things take), not the economics (how much things cost).

It's like knowing a journey takes 3 hours. Whether petrol costs ₹100 or ₹120 per litre doesn't change the driving time.

---

## Example

The AI looks at the 17 numbers and says: "This part will take about 7.5 minutes to machine."

Machining cost is then computed using the selected machine's hourly rate:

7.5 min x (selected hourly rate / 60 min)

If you switch to a different machine with a different hourly rate, the rupee cost changes even though the predicted time stays the same.

Safety detail: if the AI ever gives an impossible value (like negative minutes), we clamp it to a small minimum time so the rest of the quote doesn’t break.

---

## What does this step produce?

A single number: predicted machining time in minutes. This feeds into both the cost calculation (Step 10) and the uncertainty range (Step 9).
