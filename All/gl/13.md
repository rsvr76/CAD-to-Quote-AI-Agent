# Step 13 - The Final Quote (Grandma Version)

---

## What are we actually doing here?

Everything comes together. The engineer sees the complete, final manufacturing quote on one screen - with the total cost, a breakdown, a confidence range, explanations, and design improvement suggestions.

It's like getting a car repair estimate that shows labour, parts, taxes AND explains why the alternator needs replacing and suggests a cheaper compatible part.

---

## What does the final screen show?

### The big number

```
Total Cost: ₹28,400
```

### Where does each rupee go?

```
Material:    ₹8,200   (weight × price per kg formula)
Machining:   ₹14,500  (AI time prediction × hourly rate)
Setup:       ₹3,100   (fixed setup shared across units)
Overhead:    ₹2,600   (factory running costs)

### A quick picture: what % goes where?

Below the breakdown, the system also shows a simple chart that splits the total into percentages.

Example idea:

- Machining: 51%
- Material: 29%
- Setup: 11%
- Overhead: 9%

This makes it obvious what the biggest cost driver is before you look at the detailed explanations.

Small UI improvement: the chart can show the **total cost in the center** (like professional dashboards), and also show a quick line such as:

Largest Cost Driver: Machining (₹14,500)

The breakdown lines can also show percentages, like:

- Material: ₹8,200 (29%)
- Machining: ₹14,500 (51%)
- Setup: ₹3,100 (11%)
- Overhead: ₹2,600 (9%)
```

### Process-wise machining split (extra clarity)

Many judges/engineers ask: “Okay, machining is ₹14,500 — but *which process* is eating the time?”

So we also show a simple process-wise split of machining cost:

```
Machining breakdown (process-wise)
Process   | Machine         | Minutes | Rate (₹/hr) | Cost (₹)
----------+-----------------+---------+-------------+----------
Milling   | 5-Axis          | 3.4     | 1800        | 102.00
Drilling  | VMC Drill       | 2.5     | 900         | 37.50
Grinding  | Surface Grinder | 1.6     | 900         | 24.00
----------+-----------------+---------+-------------+----------
Total machining cost: ₹163.50
```

Notes (kept honest):
- The AI predicts one **total** machining time.
- We split that time across the processes that are ON (a reasonable rule-of-thumb), so we can apply the correct ₹/hour for each selected machine.
- If the engineer changes routing/machines, it updates and is recorded as an **Engineer override**.

### How reliable is this number?

```
Estimated Range (Machining Uncertainty): ₹24,200 – ₹31,800
(uncertainty is only in the machining portion)
```

### Why does it cost this much?

The SHAP waterfall chart, formula details, and routing decisions all appear below.

### Could it be cheaper?

The DFM suggestions are ranked by savings at the bottom.

If the engineer taps one, the system applies it as a **simulated change** (a “what-if” quote variant), recalculates the quote, and can show **Original vs Modified assumptions** side by side. There’s also a **Revert all changes** button to go back to the original.

Applying (or reverting) a DFM suggestion is tagged as an **Engineer override** because it is an intentional engineer-driven change.

### Can I download or share it?

Yes — the system can generate a simple PDF-style quote summary (like an invoice) so the engineer can share it with a manager or vendor.

And if the engineer applied a design change (DFM), the PDF export can also include a **comparison print**: two designs side by side (Original vs Modified) with their costs, so higher-ups can decide quickly.

## What makes this system special?

1. **Fast** - Under 30 seconds from upload to quote
2. **Transparent** - Every cost component shows its source (formula, AI, or rule)
3. **Explainable** - SHAP tells you WHY this specific part costs what it does
4. **Helpful** - DFM suggestions save money before you even start manufacturing

---

## The big picture

This system combines three things:

- **Engineering rules** (routing, formulas) for what's predictable
- **AI prediction** (Random Forest) for what's complex
- **Human judgment** (engineer confirmation) for what needs expertise

The result: a reliable manufacturing quote that an engineer can trust, understand, and act on.
